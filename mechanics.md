# Reaction Dynamics

The core system in the demo is the **Haber–Bosch reaction**:

N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃

This reaction is **reversible**, meaning it can proceed in both directions.

Inside the reactor, the reaction continuously moves toward a state called
**chemical equilibrium**.

At equilibrium, the forward reaction and the reverse reaction occur at the same
rate.

---

## Reaction Quotient (Q)

The current state of the gas mixture inside the reactor is described by the
**reaction quotient Q**.

Q depends on the relative amounts of nitrogen, hydrogen, and ammonia present in
the reactor.

As the reaction proceeds, Q changes over time as molecules are converted between
reactants and products.

---

## Equilibrium Constant (K)

Each reaction also has an **equilibrium constant K**, which represents the ratio
of products to reactants when the system reaches equilibrium.

For a given temperature, **K is fixed**.

The reaction naturally evolves until:

Q = K

At this point the system has reached equilibrium and the net reaction stops
changing.

---

## Reaction Direction Indicator

To help players understand the system intuitively, the reactor displays a
**reaction direction indicator**.

This indicator compares the current mixture (Q) with the equilibrium state (K).

Q < K →→→ reaction favors ammonia production

Q ≈ K ⇄ system near equilibrium

Q > K ←←← reaction favors decomposition

This visual indicator shows the **direction and strength of the reaction**
inside the reactor.

---

## Industrial Insight

In a simple reactor, the reaction will eventually stop once equilibrium is
reached, limiting ammonia production.

Industrial systems overcome this limitation by modifying the reactor conditions.

Players gradually discover how to influence the system by:

- increasing **temperature** to speed up the reaction
- adding an **iron catalyst** to accelerate the reaction
- increasing **pressure** to shift equilibrium toward ammonia
- removing ammonia through **condensation**

These modifications allow the system to continuously produce ammonia, forming
the basis of the industrial Haber–Bosch process.
